The Challenge

How do you take a successful execution of a complex operation and turn it into something that can scale when the software models used to support the well site misrepresent the actual fluid dynamics in the well?

When the model doesn't match reality

  • Added stress on the crew — the well isn't doing what the model says it should, and people are making critical decisions under pressure
  • Decisions without adequate information — you can't see in the well, and the computations are too complex to do by hand. When the model doesn't match reality, you're forced to make assumptions and calculate complex scenarios on the fly
  • Mistakes — got out of the well, but on losses — exceeded desired EMD
  • Resistance — people won't want to do it if it doesn't work reliably
The approach: Build a software model that does.

What the Software Models Were Missing

1. Slugging behavior

The existing software models did not account for the drill string float or pipe slugging. Air in the string must be replaced with mud by the time you're out of the well. Backfill was treated as pipe displacement only — no slug equalization, no intermittent draining.

2. Single-rheology assumptions

With kill mud, active mud, slugs, and spacers all in the wellbore, a single-rheology causes incorrect dynamic back pressure recommendations.

Not modeling slugging behavior and use of a single rheology, compounded to make operations unreliable and difficult to execute.

What this unlocked: kill at any depth

We knew from experience that the existing software was overestimating required kill mud density and swab pressure. Killing the well at 1000m was desired, but seemed impossible based on the previous season's results. By properly accounting for slugging and multi-fluid rheology, we could now apply kill muds in different ways — killing the well at any depth.

Float Valve Physics

When slug is pumped, its hydrostatic column eventually exceeds annulus pressure at the bit — the float cracks open and mud U-tubes into the annulus.

1. Slug Pumped

Slug pumped into string. Heavier column builds hydrostatic pressure at the float.

2. U-Tube

Float cracks open. Slug flows down the string, through the bit, and up into the annulus. Air backfills from surface.

3. Equilibrium

Pressures balanced — float closes. String is dry, slug now sits in annulus above the bit. Backfill = steel displacement + slug drop.

Key insight: Control of slugging and float state is imperative to ensure adequate surface back pressure is applied.

Fluid Blending Below the Bit

As the bit moves up during trip out, fluid flows into the growing pocket below the bit. What ends up there depends on the float.

Float Closed

Pocket = annulus fluid only
Slug stays in string — density, rheology, color from annulus

Float Open

Pocket = volume-weighted blend
Annulus + string fluids mix — density, rheology, color all blended

Coriolis validation: Density readings at surface during trips back in matched the model's predicted blended pocket density — confirming the algorithm is correct.

Intermittent Slugging & the Fill-Up Schedule

Slugging creates an air volume in the string. As you trip out, that air has to be replaced — but how much fill per interval depends on the float state and whether a slugging event occurs.

What's Happening Downhole

As pipe is pulled, pressure at the bit shifts. The float opens and closes intermittently — each interval sees a different fill requirement.

Fill Volume by Float State

The simulator tracks the float state at every interval and outputs a fill-up schedule — replacing the air volume incrementally, one stand at a time.

Swab, Surge & APL

Moving pipe creates surge and swab. Circulating creates APL. The models for both already existed — they just needed to be linked to the multi-fluid stack.

Single Rheology

One friction calculation for the whole column. Overestimates in some zones, underestimates in others.

Per-Layer Rheology

Each layer contributes friction based on its own PV/YP. Total swab or surge = sum of all layers. Eccentricity factor adjusts for pipe position.

Why this matters: The fluid distribution changes every time you pull a stand or circulate. If your friction model is wrong, your back pressure recommendation is wrong — you are either fracturing the well or going underbalanced without knowing it.

The Numerical Model: Core Concepts

Multi-layer fluid column with three compartments: String, Annulus, Pocket (below bit)

Each layer tracks density, rheology (PV/YP), volume, position, and color.

Pressure Method — Slug Displacement on Ensign 816

Reading the pressure trace

Slug pumped, then displaced with chase fluid

  • ICP 40,500 kPa — stable pump pressure while pumping slug
  • −150 kPa — SPP dips as heavier slug is harder to suck from the tank
  • +300 kPa — peak when slug reaches top of standpipe
  • +250 kPa — small U-shape as slug travels down and back up the kelly hose
  • Gradual → linear decrease — slug enters drill string, accelerating pressure drop
  • −2,800 kPa — flat — all slug is in the pipe
Key measurement: The volume pumped until pressure goes flat tells us how much chase is needed to get all the slug into the pipe. This is the rig's surface line volume.
Why it matters: If you don't account for the surface line volume, slug gets left in the surface piping and you pull wet pipe.

Circulating

A circulation model was added to track fluid displacement through the wellbore and determine correct pump rate and back pressure to maintain ECD.

Why circulation modeling matters

  • Circulating changes the fluid distribution in the wellbore
  • Kill mud gets displaced, active mud moves in
  • APL changes with the fluid composition at each depth

What the model provides

  • Pump rate vs ECD relationship with actual fluid stack
  • Required back pressure to maintain target ESD
  • Prediction of how fluid distribution changes over time
Key point: Every time you circulate, it changes the fluid distribution — which changes everything downstream.

Cementing

The numerical model's multi-fluid approach proved useful beyond tripping operations. A feature was added that supports cementing schedules with operational steps, loss zones, and pump rate recommendations.

The problem

  • Loss zone present in the well
  • Pump rate directly affects ECD and whether returns are maintained
  • Too fast → losses; too slow → poor cement job

What the feature provides

  • Cementing schedule with operational steps and fluid volumes
  • Loss zone awareness — pump rate recommendations to maintain returns
  • Multi-layer rheology accounts for cement, spacer, and mud interactions
Result: The same numerical approach that models tripping can model cementing — any operation that moves fluids through a multi-layer wellbore.

Trip In Simulation

Why it was needed

We had the math and model for tripping out that we believed in, but we had to rely on the existing software model for tripping in — which seemed to underestimate surge pressure.

  • After tripping out, what happens when you trip back in with a different string size?
  • Where are the fluids at each depth?
  • When do you go underbalanced?
Limitation:The trip in simulation inherits the fluid state from the trip out sim but was tedious to do.

What the trip-in model tracks

  • Layer expansion — fluid layers expand vertically as fluid enters pipe annulus
  • Displacement returns to surface
  • ESD at control depth at every step
  • Required choke pressure to maintain target ESD
  • Surge pressure with per-layer rheology

Kill Mud Optimization

By this point, several successful trips had been made using the model — in and out, at depths from the heel to the toe.

Model showed the problem

Model showed an increase in EMD 10–20 kg/m³ higher at surface than target.

Model validation

Fill volumes matched actual rig trip sheet volumes — confirming accuracy. No increase in flare size on return to bottom.

More kill mud than they thought possible

  • Old software models limited kill mud to pipe displacement volume — slugging was not modeled
  • Model showed that slug equalization gets ~4 m³ more kill mud into the well
  • Use more but lighter kill mud — was told it was not possible, you cannot place that much mud in the well
  • On every trip, the trip record sheet showed fill volume was equivalent to pipe volume + slug drop volume — assisted by the use of the Iron Maiden and Mud Dog

Then reduced the density

  • Once volumes were proved, started reducing kill mud density to stay closer to target pressure
  • Closer to target = less risk of exceeding fracture pressure

Density Optimization

The problem

Using full kill mud for the entire backfill can cause you to exceed the desired EMD. Could switch to lighter active mud partway through the trip.

The model showed

  • How active mud near the end of the backfill can help with pulling dry pipe

Ream In/Out

Reaming combines pipe movement and circulation at the same time — surge/swab and APL are happening simultaneously.

Why it needed its own model

  • Pipe movement creates surge or swab while the pumps are also generating APL
  • The combined effect on bottomhole pressure is not simply additive — fluid distribution is changing as you ream
  • Needed for tight spots where you cannot just trip — you have to ream through

What was added

  • Ream in and ream out model with full fluid state tracking
  • Simultaneous pipe speed + pump rate inputs
  • Continuous ESD and back pressure output through the ream interval

Super Simulation: Chaining Operations

Real operations are not isolated

A trip involves: trip out, hit tight hole, pump/circulate, continue tripping, circulate at shoe, trip back in. Each operation changes the fluid distribution, which changes everything downstream.

What Super Simulation does

  • Chains any sequence: Trip Out → Circulate → Trip In → Ream In → Ream Out
  • Carries the complete fluid state (all layers, densities, rheologies) between operations
  • Shows continuous ESD, back pressure, and pump rate across all operations
  • Enables "what if" planning: what happens if I need to circulate at 2000m?

Example: Tight Hole During Trip Out

1. Tripping out with slug and backfill

Well is dead at 300m. Everything going to plan.

2. Hit tight hole at 2500m — need to ream

Cannot trip freely. Ream through the tight spot — this changes the fluid distribution.

3. Circulate

Pump to clean hole and restore circulation. Kill mud gets displaced, kill zone retreats.

4. Reset slug and backfill

Re-establish the fluid state to where it was before reaming. Slug and backfill picks up from the new starting point.

5. Continue tripping

Resume trip out with updated fluid distribution, back pressure, and fill schedule.

Without the model: You don't know what the reaming and circulation did to the fluid distribution, or what back pressure to use going forward.

With the model: Chain the events in Super Sim — ream, circulate, reset, continue. The fluid state carries through every step.

New Challenge: Pressure, Torque & Hook Load

These models exist, but their inputs are limited and cannot capture all wellbore states. Can we connect them to the numerical model and deliver a more accurate, prediction?

Standpipe Pressure

  • Existing models use a single mud weight — during displacement, the fluid stack is changing. SPP should change with it
  • Laminar-only friction — at typical drilling flow rates, flow is turbulent. Laminar models underestimate SPP by 40–60%
  • No geometry awareness — a tapered string changes annular velocity at every section boundary

Torque & Hook Load

  • Single buoyancy factor — with multiple fluids in the well, buoyancy varies along the string
  • No circulating effects — APL and surface back pressure change the load on the string
  • Static predictions — hook load and torque should update as the fluid state changes during the operation
The approach: Hook 'em up and see what happens.

What it computes

  • Pickup, slackoff, rotating hook load and free-hanging weight
  • Surface torque — rotary torque at surface for rotating on/off bottom
  • Rotating hoist/slackoff torque — combined rotation + axial motion with velocity-ratio friction splitting
  • String weight reduction from circulating effects (APL piston force) and application of surface back pressure
  • Pressure-area buoyancy — accounts for different fluids inside and outside the string, and varying cross-sections along a tapered string
Calibration: Friction factors tuned per section until sim matches rig data. Once calibrated, the model predicts hook loads and torque at any depth.

Standpipe Pressure

SPP = String Friction + Bit Nozzle ΔP + Annulus Friction + Surface Back Pressure

How it works

  • Power-law rheology per fluid layer from Fann 35 readings or PV/YP
  • Turbulent flow detection via Metzner-Reed Reynolds number with Dodge-Metzner friction factor
  • Bit nozzle ΔP from total flow area and fluid density
  • Geometry-split integration — friction split at casing/hole and pipe section boundaries

Why it matters

  • Higher than expected — pack-off, plugged nozzle, formation change
  • Lower than expected — washout, lost nozzle, lost circulation
  • Changing during displacement — expected if the model accounts for it; a problem if it doesn't
Current status: Model currently appears to be out by the surface line friction only — the pressure loss from the standpipe to the kelly hose is not yet included.
